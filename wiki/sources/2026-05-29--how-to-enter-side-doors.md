# How to enter side doors

## Summary
Maja's Velvet Noise essay argues that early-career job search should not be treated only as submitting applications through crowded front doors. The useful frame is that a job is a bundle of problems someone wants solved, and side doors work when a candidate makes their usefulness legible to people with live problems through specificity, cold outreach, and public artifacts. The essay connects career search to [[Hacker Mindset]] and [[Abstraction-leak Reasoning]]: official roles and application funnels are surface abstractions over people, budgets, bottlenecks, trust, and proof. Evidence is medium as practitioner framing but weak as general outcome proof because the career examples are anecdotal, survivorship-biased, and dependent on timing, networks, taste, and luck.

## Key Claims
1. The job market can be modeled as a search problem: employers seek signal about who can solve messy problems, and applicants seek people or organizations whose problems they can help solve.
2. Official job postings are a lossy interface, not the full system; many possible roles begin as unscoped problems, informal conversations, or one person taking a bet.
3. Side doors have outbound and inbound forms: specific direct outreach to people/companies, and public artifacts that let relevant people discover how someone thinks.
4. Specificity is signal. A cold email, memo, teardown, essay, guide, prototype, or analysis is more useful when it shows the sender has studied the recipient's context and can make a small, concrete contribution.
5. Public proof-of-work can travel ahead of the person, making their thinking findable to the small subset of people tuned to the same problem space.
6. The approach is not a fairness guarantee: most attempts may fail, and outcomes are affected by luck, privilege, timing, networks, and taste.

## Notable Quotes
- "A job is a bundle of problems someone wants solved badly enough to pay another person to solve them."
- "A job is one way they ask for help. A side door is another way of offering it."
- "Specificity is one of the highest forms of respect."
- "Post online and get acquired by Canva" is explicitly rejected as bad advice; the narrower lesson is that work close to a problem can become a beacon for people who recognize it.
- "The goal is to create a small, undeniable unit of signal."

## Evidence and Caveats
- Medium evidence for the framing: the essay is coherent practitioner synthesis and aligns with prior Tolaria notes on hacker mindset and abstraction leaks.
- Weak evidence for outcome reliability: anecdotes do not prove that cold outreach, public writing, or visible artifacts reliably produce jobs across fields or backgrounds.
- Strongest counterargument: side-door success stories are likely survivorship-biased and may understate privilege, existing networks, platform luck, social taste, and the recipient's discretionary power.
- Leonardo.ai/cofounder and acquisition claims should remain parent-source/anecdotal until corroborated by independent primary or high-credibility sources.
