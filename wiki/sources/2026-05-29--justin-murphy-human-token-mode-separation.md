# Justin Murphy X post on human-maxxing vs token-maxxing

## Summary
Justin Murphy argues that AI-era knowledge work works better as a barbell: pure human-only blocks for reading, thinking, and writing, and separate token-heavy machine/AI blocks for execution. His example routine uses different times and physical spaces: library in the morning for human work, gym as a transition, and a later afternoon block for "psychotically machinic" AI/tool use. The source is useful as a compact hypothesis about [[Human-Token Mode Separation]], but it is weak evidence because it is a personal anecdote with no benchmark, primary source, external link, reproducible method, or comparison against integrated AI-assisted workflows. Its best Tolaria use is as a vocabulary and open question that can be compared with [[Context Engineering]], [[Dynamic Context Loading]], and [[Anthropic Building Effective Agents and Hermes Workflow Implications]].

## Key Claims
1. The "new barbell" is to be either purely human-maxxing or purely token-maxxing, with the blended middle framed as the worst mode.
2. The separation is not trivial; Murphy says it requires reflection and purposeful reorganization of life against existing habits.
3. Physical-space and time-of-day boundaries may make the two states easier to preserve.
4. Trying to use AI while doing "meaningfully human" work can feel confused and produce poor, inefficient results in both modes.
5. The claim is anecdotal and should remain a hypothesis, not an adopted workflow rule.

## Notable Quotes
> "The new barbell is to be purely human-maxxing or purely token-maxxing, but nothing in between. The between is the worst."

> "I’m finding the more I separate the two states of mind with different physical spaces and times of day, the better things work and feel."

> "If you’re trying to use AI for anything while you’re also trying to do anything meaningfully human, I have generally found that it’s a terribly confused and frustrating feeling and it produces poor, inefficient results on both."
