# There's Always a Door

## Summary
Maja's Velvet Noise essay argues that people often accept a frame as the full possibility space: table or grass, mother or writer, ambition or peace, front door or no door. The reusable idea is that the missing option may sit outside the current crop, not inside the offered dichotomy. The essay supports a soft cognitive layer adjacent to [[Side Doors]], [[Hacker Mindset]], and [[Abstraction-leak Reasoning]]: official frames, inherited maps, and environmental incentives can hide legitimate moves without proving that all constraints are fake. Evidence is weak because the article is a personal/philosophical practitioner essay with anecdotes and unlinked references rather than empirical support.

## Key Claims
1. Many everyday choices are framed as false dichotomies; asking "why not both?" or "where is the door?" can expose options the original question excluded.
2. Frames can be useful scaffolds, but they also shape which questions seem askable, which identities seem allowed, and which possibilities disappear.
3. Cropping is the central metaphor: what is outside the visible frame can be real even when it is not currently salient.
4. Internal maps and "reality tunnels" are incomplete constructions shaped by prior experience, incentives, language, and social games.
5. The practical move is repeated reframing: question the offered frame, check the map against the territory, and look for legitimate options outside the crop.

## Notable Quotes
- "My question had assumed a constraint that didn’t exist."
- "The most dangerous ideas can be not the ones being argued, but the ones assumed."
- "The crop defines what’s real."
- "Most possibilities live outside the crop."
- "So: question your frames. Check your maps. Look for the door, even if you can’t yet see it."
