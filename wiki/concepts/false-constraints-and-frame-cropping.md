# False Constraints and Frame-cropping

## Definition
False Constraints and Frame-cropping is the cognitive pattern of mistaking the current frame, question, menu, map, or visible crop for the whole possibility space, then recovering agency by asking what legitimate options the frame excluded.

## Scope
This concept covers everyday and strategic reframing: spotting false dichotomies, asking whether a choice can be both/and rather than either/or, checking whether a perceived missing door is merely outside the visible crop, and revising internal maps when incentives or identity scripts have narrowed imagination. It does not mean all constraints are fake; law, consent, safety, budget, trust, credentials, and physical limits can be real walls.

## Contrasts
- Versus [[Side Doors]]: side doors are a career/opportunity route; false-constraint detection is the broader cognitive move that may reveal a side door or another legitimate option.
- Versus [[Hacker Mindset]]: hacker mindset often inspects the lower-level substrate beneath an official system; frame-cropping is softer and asks whether the offered framing already excluded the relevant option.
- Versus [[Abstraction-leak Reasoning]]: abstraction-leak reasoning maps surface objects to implementation mechanics; false-constraint reframing maps visible options to the wider possibility space and asks what the crop hid.
- Versus wishful thinking: reframing is useful only when it tests the frame against reality instead of declaring every unwanted limitation imaginary.

## Evidence
- [[There's Always a Door]] is the direct practitioner source. It gives the table-or-grass, Nighthawks-with-no-visible-door, photography crop, reality-tunnel, social-game, and map-not-territory metaphors for noticing omitted options.
- Evidence is low: the source is conceptual and anecdotal. It is useful as vocabulary and a caution against false dichotomies, not as empirical proof that reframing changes outcomes.
