# Side Doors

## Definition
Side Doors are career-entry or opportunity-creation paths that route around crowded official job funnels by making a person's relevance visible to someone with a live problem, usually through specific outreach, warm introductions, public artifacts, or small units of proof.

## Scope
The concept covers ethical, consent-respecting ways to surface useful signal: specific cold emails, founder/employee conversations, thoughtful memos, teardowns, essays, prototypes, guides, datasets, communities, or other artifacts that help a recipient imagine working with the sender. It does not mean spamming busy people, bypassing security or consent boundaries, pretending expertise, or treating every posted requirement as fake.

## Contrasts
- Versus front-door applications: front doors use formal postings, applicant tracking systems, resumes, and standardized screens; side doors rely more on specific context, trust, timing, and demonstrated usefulness.
- Versus [[Hacker Mindset]]: hacker mindset is the broader stance of seeing beneath official abstractions; side doors are one career-strategy application of that stance.
- Versus [[Making Usefulness Legible]]: side doors are the route; usefulness-legibility is the signal that makes the route plausible.
- Versus entitlement: a side door should reduce the recipient's interpretive burden, not demand attention without proof.

## Evidence
