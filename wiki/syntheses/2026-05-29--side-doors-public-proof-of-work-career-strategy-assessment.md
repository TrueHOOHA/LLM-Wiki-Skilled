# Side Doors and Public Proof-of-work Career Strategy Assessment

## Question / Purpose
Assess [[How to enter side doors]] as a practitioner source about [[Side Doors]], [[Jobs as Search Problems]], [[Public Proof-of-work]], [[Making Usefulness Legible]], and [[Learning in Public as Credential]], while connecting it cautiously to the existing [[Hacker Mindset]] and [[Abstraction-leak Reasoning]] cluster.

## Answer / Analysis
Strongest counterargument first: the article is not proof that cold outreach or public artifacts reliably beat formal applications. It is a conceptual/career-advice essay supported by anecdotes, and successful side-door stories are especially vulnerable to survivorship bias: failed emails, invisible privilege, existing networks, timing, platform distribution, and recipient discretion disappear from the story.

The durable Tolaria payload is still useful: official job funnels are lossy interfaces. A job posting is one representation of a company's needs, but the underlying substrate is people with problems, budgets, bottlenecks, attention constraints, trust judgments, and unscoped work. Side doors work, when they work, by creating a different kind of signal: specificity, small proof, direct relevance, or a public artifact that lets a problem-owner inspect how the candidate thinks.

This connects cleanly to the prior hacker-mindset cluster. [[How to walk through walls]] and [[On Seeing Through and Unseeing: The Hacker Mindset]] both warn that surface ontologies are incomplete maps. [[How to enter side doors]] applies that frame to hiring: the surface ontology is roles, requirements, applications, and credentials; the lower-level system is people trying to get work done under uncertainty. The safe lesson is not "ignore official channels" but "model the underlying problem system and make useful signal easier to see."

[[There's Always a Door]] adds a weaker but useful companion frame: sometimes the missing route is not a lower-level substrate but an option cropped out by the question itself. Tolaria should preserve that as [[False Constraints and Frame-cropping]] while keeping it archive-level for action: it is a reminder to inspect the frame, not evidence that every constraint can be bypassed.

## Implications
- Treat formal applications and side-door work as complementary, not mutually exclusive.
- Favor specificity over volume: study the target, identify a plausible problem, and make a small, inspectable unit of signal.
- Public artifacts are strongest when they are useful to a knowledgeable audience, not merely polished for generic attention.
- For Tolaria/Hermes thinking, this is a cognitive/research frame only; it does not authorize any workflow, outreach, scraping, hiring, or automation change.
- Treat "there is always a door" as a search prompt, not as a guarantee: real constraints still include consent, safety, law, budget, trust, credentials, and physical limits.
