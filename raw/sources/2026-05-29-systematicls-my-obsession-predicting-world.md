Title: My Obsession With Predicting The World

Introduction

Recent conversations have left me thinking about my motivations, and the driving force behind the hours I put into my work at the expense of nearly everything else.

I don't think I've ever written about this publicly before. For as long as I've had ambition, I've been driven by a desire to build a world model so adept at predicting the world that it can drive out inefficiencies in all markets, public or private.

If you've ever watched Westworld, yes, I'm talking about something like Rehoboam: a superintelligence that could map human behavior and predict the future. Interestingly enough, Rehoboam started out as a model for predicting stock prices (really).

It's a large part of why being a "quant" has interested me. The markets are the ultimate testing field for your ability to predict the future across various timescales, and it's an immensely scalable endeavor with a positive flywheel: the better you are at predictions, the more resources you get to become better at predictions, and so on.

I Have A Dream

One of my fears is that I'll die before reaching my grand ambition of predicting all the useful things society needs predictions about, and be forever immortalized as just another human obsessed with making money for the sake of making money.

My reason for being is that I believe it's possible to build an entity so adept at predictions that it can minimize inefficiencies across all human systems. That we can build a world model that guides humanity toward decisions better than heuristics or random guesses.

This means we can fund the startup ideas with the greatest predicted impact, issue scholarships to children with nascent genius. For example: which president should be elected to increase the probability of global peace and prosperity?

I think humans are in a race against time. As a species, we have finite time to expand beyond the limits of our current prison. When facing finite resources, we need to maximize efficiency to improve our odds of reaching the next bound before we run out and perish. If we never figure out how to escape Earth before resources are consumed, or before conditions make leaving impossible, we'll inevitably die as a species in a prison of our own making.

Quoted poem excerpt from Walt Whitman, "O Me! O Life!" included in source.

The author writes that they hope their verse will be "an entity that makes humanity more efficient, that nudges our probability of reaching the next bound up a little."

Predictions

The author says they have been obsessed with predictions since they were a teen, citing physics as an example of simple models producing profound predictive implications. They argue systems are modeled as random when predictions are no better than guessing because insufficient information is available.

Example given: coin flips appear 50/50 without additional information, but with the initial face, height, force, dimensions, mass, and landing material, a model could predict the landing face more accurately than random.

The author says they believe determinism has limits, but human systems have barely scratched the surface because relevant data was historically sparse or unstructured and the models/compute needed to find generalizable high-dimensional patterns were unavailable.

They claim the principle that more relevant data yields better predictions given a sufficiently powerful model holds even as systems grow more complex. An entity trying to predict the world would need to accumulate as much data as possible; recent LLM scaling suggests intelligence can emerge from sufficient scale and complexity in data and models.

Machine Learning And Higher Dimensions

The author argues data is inert without a model; model choice is non-trivial because of the no-free-lunch theorem; and modern ML lets data dictate model form rather than imposing a hand-written theory.

They claim large ML models can discover high-dimensional generalizable patterns in repeated behavior. Example: using Substacks an entrepreneur follows or podcasts they subscribe to as features to predict probability of startup success before incorporation, because tangential data may reveal beliefs, drives, and worldviews.

The author speculates about learning a person's potential from childhood experiences, beliefs, attributes, and character before adulthood, then allocating capital to otherwise wasted genius.

Conclusion

The author concludes that building such systems requires vast data and compute, but data/compute are getting cheaper and LLMs make unstructured data less problematic. They claim society is getting good at training huge "god models" that learn high-dimensional patterns.

They argue such scale needs a business that can sequentially finance its ambitions; starting with liquid-market predictions is the obvious choice before moving to harder markets and systems. They connect this path to their current work and say that if they die while running something that resembles a cash grab, it was not meant to be the destination.
