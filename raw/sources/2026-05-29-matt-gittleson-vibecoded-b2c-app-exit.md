## Article text captured from X DOM

Title: “I vibecoded a B2C app and exited for $375,000 in 6 months (full guide)”

The article claims the author went from idea to a $375,000 acquisition in six months, without a team or coding ability, by building a web app around a distribution/content engine rather than a mobile app. He says the project was built nights and weekends around a 9-9 consulting job and that the exit let him become a full-time founder and move to Korea to work at Jenni AI.

Core claims and operating lessons preserved from the article:

- Making content is framed as the best way to get acquired for the highest possible multiple, and as a learnable skill.
- AI tooling is described as having weakened the technical moat; the moat is said to have moved to distribution.
- A solo nontechnical builder with a laptop and a system can now build, scale with UGC, and sell a B2C app quickly.
- Acquirers are said to buy attention/distribution they cannot build themselves, not only product features.
- Examples named in the article: MyFitnessPal buying Cal AI; Quizlet buying Coconote and Answers AI; Halo AI’s prank format; UMax face-rating; CiteSure; Jenni AI.
- The guide recommends starting with a viral content format before choosing/building the app: pick a format that is already working on TikTok, can support 50+ variations, and has survived at least one fatigue cycle.
- It recommends studying apps already winning or borrowing proven viral formats from ecommerce/info products, then replacing weak products with higher-LTV recurring apps.
- MVP advice: work backwards from the content format; build only the minimum app needed to satisfy the format. For a “scan → score/reveal” format, the app mainly needs to do the scan and reveal well. The author recommends Cursor / Claude Code rather than overly simplified builders that limit control, and says the MVP should be live within two weeks of choosing the format.
- Retention warning: content formats that drive one-and-done app usage create churn. The best formats route into products people use daily/weekly/habitually.
- Content production advice: for the first 100 videos, make the videos yourself rather than using agents, AI influencers, automation, or hired creators. Rationale: founders need to build taste/viral sense before delegating or automating.
- Posting system claimed: 3 posts daily across multiple accounts on TikTok/Reels/Shorts; track prediction skill; optimize trending view average rather than random viral hits.
- Production system claimed: every post made by the founder; marketing spend under $100; 30M views; peak $132k ARR; $375k exit. The author describes 50 hook clips, max 3 uses per demo, constant re-filming of demos, hook clips sourced from Fiverr/Upwork or friends/family, and Sunday batch filming.
- TikTok anti-duplication is attributed partly to perceptual hashing; reusing demos too often is said to throttle reach/shadowban videos.
- Content portfolio rule claimed: 50% proven formats, 25% iterations on those formats, 25% moonshot experiments.
- Posting volume rule claimed: 3 posts/day/account maximum; more is said to reduce average views, especially on TikTok.
- Viral-sense training: scroll 30 minutes/day inside the niche and 30 minutes/day outside the niche.
- Organic vs paid claim: organic UGC is described as an asset that can continue to drive signups/MRR after posting stops. The author says CiteSure grew after acquisition despite no new videos in almost a year: acquired around £75k/$100k ARR after a $132k ARR peak; later at £135k/$180k ARR.
- Retention and multiple: article gives a 50% churn example to argue high churn lowers acquisition multiples and requires constant replacement MRR. Suggested churn reducers: better product, painkiller framing, cancellation-flow value education, retention incentives, understanding customer psychology.
- Acquisition advice: seek strategic buyers for higher multiples; inbound is preferred; marketplaces named include Acquire, TrustMRR, Flippa, Microns; brokers named include Empire Flippers; outbound requires ideal acquirer profiling and cold outreach.
- Acquisition story claimed: Jenni team first reached out to one of the accounts he was running for a content partnership, thinking it was a creator account; this became an acquisition conversation.
- Closing message: do not skip the uncomfortable work of making hundreds of posts; building the operator/distribution skill is the asset.
