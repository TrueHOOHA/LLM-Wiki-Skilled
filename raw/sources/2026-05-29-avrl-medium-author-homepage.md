```text
Data Storage system deep dive
 How storage system work in detail
 article banner 
 Since the advent of Computer Science in early 1950s, storage systems (non-volatile) remained an unavoidable component of all systems. All our data, be it cloud, on premise, or on your own devices, is stored in these storage systems since the start of the time. Primarily, we have two type of storage systems, volatile and non-volatile storage .
 Non-volatile systems include all caches & RAM , including CPU registers & other distributed caches like Redis . (ik ik redis has a append-only persistence feature) These systems are faster to Read/Write with but lose their state as their power is cut. That’s why we use non-volatile systems to store data without power. These are much slower than volatile systems, but persist data forever. We have had different types of non-volatile storage systems over different generations, but broadly they are of three types:
 Tape storage 
 Rotating Disk storage (HDD) 
 Solid State Storage (SSD) 

 (A surprise if you read till end!) 
 1. Tape Storage
 tape storage architecture 
 Tape Storage is one of the earliest ways (1950s) we used to store non-volatile data. They were of varied sizes, from filling a room to the cassettes you used in your Walkman and cassette players. The basic structure of a tape reader was designed to read tape cartridges inserted into them.
 Each tape cartridge (cassette) have two sides A and B on which the data is encoded, and when inserted in the reader, the tape gets wounded and unwounded from both sides over the IO head , which reads and writes the data.
 The Tape is a long magnetic reel which has many tracks running in parallel & each track has small cells (blocks where binary data is stored). Each cell can have its magnetic polarisation set to 1 or 0 in binary. Technically, the magnetic field created by the transition between two cells is what makes the 1 or zero. A sequence of bits on a tape forms a page of data, usually 4k or 8k bytes long.
 Read and Write Process 
 typical tape R/W process (by author) 
 The IO head reads/writes from/to the tape as the motors on both sides of the tape reader move to get to the cell where the data is. Tape storage is essentially slower in cases when the R/W happens farther the head of the reader & faster when it is near to it. This is because the tape needs to be spun for a long distance before it reaches the correct cell to R/W.
 Even though the speed of the motors are very fast, reaching upto 100s of inches per second, the length of the tape & the constant back and forth to find the correct cell on the correct track is too slow for modern standards. On the flip side, since each bit is placed one after the other, tape storages are very fast & efficient in R/W’ing sequential data as it’s contiguous. Consider it like an array where each element is next to each other in the memory so contiguous/sequential operations are fast.
 Usages It’s still being used today to store cold data that’s not used, because tape storage is dirt cheap and used to store legacy/archived data (cold) that’s not queried by systems like healthcare, government and also youtube to store old videos from 15 years ago.
 But overall from modern standards, we can’t use them in active data (hot) as it’s too slow & latent to work with. Hence, we move on to the next advancement in the systems.
 2. Hard Disk Storage (HDD)
 typical HDD architecture 
 The next update came as Hard Disk Drives where instead of tape, the main unit for data storage is one or more rotating metal disk(s) called Platters. 
 Data is stored in concentric tracks on these platters and accessing a particular block requires physically moving the IO head to the correct track and waiting for the platter to rotate to the correct position . To relate both tape and hard disks, you can think that tapes are straight and hard disk curve those tapes into a circle. The hierarchy goes like this: 
 Platter -> track -> sector (page) -> 4k/8k of data (generally) 
 The platters rotate pretty fast, commonly at 7200RPM, so the operations are pretty fast and efficient.
 The platters rotate pretty fast and the IO head moves over these platters to read the correct sector from the correct track. Unlike Tapes, HDDs support random access as the entire platter is open and just needs to align with the head position. This simplification reduces the time to read/write on the platter & significantly reduces latency (~1–3ms).
 We see similar behaviour with HDDs in case of sequential data that it’s faster because the R/W happens in the same order and the IO head doesn’t need to move (seek time) and there’s no waiting for the platter to rotate to correct sector (rotational latency).
 But again, for random R/Ws, the operations take longer due to higher seek times and rotational latency.
 Imagine the IO head moving back and forth over the disk just to align with the correct track & the platter rotating to get the correct sector to head which is on opposite side of the head.
 typical HDD platter 
 Usages 
 Even though HDDs aren’t the fastest storage method, they are most used storage systems due to their speed vs cost ratio and efficiency. HDDs remain dominant in scenarios where large amounts of data need to be stored economically, such as data lakes, backup systems, and bulk storage tiers in cloud environments.
 Enhancements and cons 
 typical hdd R/W process (by author) 
 To enhance performance in HDDs, the disks support command queuing which can run in parallel, minimising the two factors of latency, seek time and rotational latency.
 The way the disk controller would schedule these commands is that it will try to cover the most amount of sectors with minimal head movements & rotation needed to carry out those operations.
With these moving parts, comes some reliability issues too. HDDs are prone to damage due to any of the component getting damaged, from the platter itself to the controller motor controlling the head.
 Also, temperature, vibrations & other external factors cause HDDs to perform worse than optimal. This is why modern systems often use redundancy mechanisms such as RAID configurations to mitigate data loss risks.
 Overall, HDDs provide a solid way to store and query data, but they are still a level slower for random R/Ws, which brings us to the SSDs, that are the widely adopted storage systems in Modern DBs and data centers.
 3. Solid State Storage (SSD)
 typical SSD architecture 
 The SSDs, or Flash storage currently dominate the storage space. But they aren’t new to be honest, they were invented in 1980s but weren’t brought into mainstream due to cost and limitations to adopt it back then, when tape and HDDs dominated the market and the stack was designed around them so fitting SSDs wasn’t possible, but it changed as our tech landscape evolved and data became a bottleneck.
 The biggest advantage of SSDs is that they don’t have any mechanical moving parts for the whole read/write of data and totally rely on a main electronic component called a NAND Flash .
So that means we can read and write data on the disk electronically with no mechanical latency & almost the speed of electricity. (Pretty fast!)
 Now the basic structure of SSDs have the following hierarchical components: Each NAND Flash module is a Target , where each target has many blocks and each block has several pages. The controller handles the operations and some SSDs have a D-RAM that contains the store mapping tables (data location index), significantly increasing drive speed and responsiveness. The controller operates each R/W using the circuit lines connecting controller and the targets called “ Traces ”.
 Read and Write Process 
 typical SSD R/W process (by author) 
 So each Target (NAND Flash) has one line ( Trace ) going from the Controller to it. This lines takes the operation signals, but only one Page can be read or written at one time by a line. This has some I/O implications that may effect the performance of the SSDs in some ways, those are SSD Garbage Collection and Parallelism .
 Since SSDs don’t have any moving parts, the performance boils down to data organisation and operations on it. This is where SSD Parallelism comes in. Each line can easily process operations in parallel to other lines with no blocking. This is like parallel roads having separate traffic on them.
 Parallelism works only when the operations are spread across targets with data operated on also not on same page inside the blocks. But if the operations are on the same line and working with the same page/block, the operations are blocked until the one in front gets executed, and that degrades performance & latency as the rest of the lines/targets sit with nothing to do.
 (You probably don’t care as a dev, but someone building a DB has to, their job is to structure data & organise it on disk in a way to minimise this same-line operations & increase parallelism as much as possible) .
 The second thing is Garbage collection . 
The smallest “ unit ” of data that can be R/W’ed on the disk is the size of the page. Nothing less is possible. While reading isn’t a problem, when you write to a page, it cannot be re-written to unless it deletes the existing data on it.
 What’s more complicated, that you cannot delete a single page, you need to delete the whole block & then you can reuse the pages in it, which is a problem for performance. For managing all of these pages with write/deletes, the SSDs have the algorithm called Garbage Collector. 
 This kicks in when you have more data to write and their aren’t enough pages to write to.
 So Garbage Collector , first marks the whole page dirty, then delete the whole block (as written above) & then write the new data to them erased pages. This significantly hampers the performance of the SSDs, but not enough to match HDDs.
 Cons 
The Garbage collection , when ran several times, can lead to degraded lifespans of SSDs & performance, described as Write Amplification. It occurs because SSDs must erase entire blocks before writing new data, forcing internal data movement and rewriting.
 The WAF ratio is the number of extra writes the SSD has to do in order to perform one real write operation. The ratio should be 1 ideally, but it can go way above 1 when the SSDs are nearly full.
 To mitigate this, SSDs do over-provisioning (extra unused space that users don’t see from outside), efficiently written controller & commands that inform beforehand which data needs to removed (eg. TRIM Command ). High write amplification is a primary reason why random write workloads reduce the lifespan of an SSD more quickly than sequential writes, because the more NAND switches flip, the more weary they get internally.
 4. Conclusion
 This visualisation shows how data flows through all 3 of the storage systems and their average time for any R/W operation. (can vary in different conditions)
 visualisation of all 3 storage types: internal working 
 Each storage type serves it purpose with their pros and cons. SSDs are the fastest even with their issues but are costliest amongst all of them, that’s why they are used in places where high-performance data transactions are critical.
 Even though volatile memory (RAMs and CPU caches) are 500x faster in data processing (R/W), you cannot load all of the data in the memory coz it’s not feasible at such scale & load.
There is much deeper we can go into how each of the components work, but this is enough for any software developer to start with on how their data is stored on different storages.
```

```text
Agile doesn’t fit the AI coding era
 AI exposed agile as it’s not working anymore
 How agile development changed with AI (by author) 
 AI has changed the whole software engineering scene within 2 years and now we are witnessing the collapse of the old norms of how we think about a process at companies for building and developing software.
 But let’s recap why agile and why it became the biggest standard of Software Development Lifecycle (SDLC).
 Agile vs Waterfall: A Recap
 Waterfall followed a linear approach to SDLC where one step happened only after the previous step was done and complete. It felt like adopted assembly line process where people worked on the same line one after the other and assembled a car.
 The problem occured because software engineering was nothing like the traditional industrial processes in industrial revolution. Fast forward to 1990s, it became clear that we needed a better standardised process for SDLC because waterfall failed as software became prevalent and mainstream with globalisation. Agile offered more flexibility, smaller feedback cycles from stakeholders and better development experience for the team with constant improvement.
 So what’s happening exactly, how does agile and AI don’t work together, when Agile was the mostly adopted standard for more than 2 decades across orgs.
 Increase of Work Density
 a typical agile schedule before AI (by author) 
 AI has drastically reduced the stack of work and people needed to do the same amount of work before 2023. The work done by 10 people can now be done by 5–6 people, yes, and I am doing it too at my job more work than ever.
 Also, every developer has become a side-tester and QA on their own to test whatever they get from their agentic loops to make sure the stuff works, also devs are more doing more work then ever and pushing out stuff faster and in larger quantities than ever. (Not anecdotal, this is the truth).
 What was earlier a 2 weeks task can now be done in 3–4 days if done efficiently. AI has shortened the time for the same work but increased the amount of it for devs because the orgs want them to be faster and do more in the same time.
 current agile dev landscape with AI (by author) 
 This doesn’t fit well with agile.
 Agile slows more than it helps today
 So what do I mean by that? 
 Agile was built for better feedback and faster SDLC to ship product faster and better, but within human limitations. A human can only work so much on different things before they no longer can properly without losing attention.
 There is no such limits for AI (actually humans are still the bottleneck but still, we are able to do much more now.) 
 Agentic coding has made devs do the real stuff that matters by delegating the boring stuff to AI. Developers are doing more testing, verification and making sure things are not breaking by being a orchestrator & from a design perspective, they are doing the jobs of architects now rather than writing all of the code by themselves. Also, agile involves a lot of meetings and discussions!
 Every day we have a standup, then sprint retrospective, review, planning, and what not, all of that stretched beyond 15 minutes and 45 minutes of recommended times for no reason because someone wanted to discuss something not important to the agenda. This all took valuable time and every dev knows how bad and boring these stakeholder meetings are, unless you are not on the centre stage.
 And don’t get me started on effort estimations & story points! 
I had to write a 2 pages answer in my exams explaining the effort estimation for a sprint planning based on story points & the fibonacci method. (Yes I hate it!) 
 These rituals were just there to make sure scrum masters and product owners have their titles and jobs. The Story points and the T-shirt size BS had no relation to time and effort on ground whatsoever.
 And the people who work on it decide them, and nobody never said the time it would actually take, it was always gamed, and once gamed, according to Goodhart’s law , it’s not a measure anymore.
 And we did this every 2–3 weeks, rinse and repeat.
 Agile was meant for human limits with no tools like AI; limited scope, fixed context awareness and time crunches with constant eyes on progress & feedbacks. Agile wasn’t meant to adapt for a technology like AI.
 AI exposed it completely.
 The Fluff-off mindset!
 The ownership and agency has been reduced to less headcount and now the fluff of people like Product Owners, Scrum Masters and Middle Managers just doesn’t sit well in this AI era.
 An architect can easily do most of the work the middle management buffers did, on their own because the overhead of management and coordination is completely changed now from back in the days.
 It made sense then because planning was difficult then & coordination was complex due to more layers and people in the loops.
 This extra boost from AI to make devs 2x more able has exposed this fluff & everyone knows it’s not needed that much anymore. The fluff around each dev writing code, from scrum masters to product owners, and middle managers, AI has made it all obsolete & impossible to justify.
 Welcome back Waterfall!
 Now traditional waterfall was garbage, it had its limitations as I already mentioned, it couldn’t adapt to the rapidly growing software demand and development as orgs grew and teams expanded. Same way Agile is now unable to adapt to AI.
 So why do I say welcome back waterfall. If you think about it, how do we interact with AI? We give a plan to execute, or a link to a JIRA/Github issue with all the details, and it creates a todo list and executes it. Maybe we plan with it, refine the plan and then let it implement it.
 Then we see the output, do some cleaning, make sure to test it rigorously and then raise a PR for it. Do you see the similarity here? Each dev is doing a mini waterfall process in their own way.
 Plan before -> implement -> test -> fix -> merge 
 how teams are developing and collaborating with ai today in teams (by author) 
 Agile was meant to be a collaborative manifesto within team to output faster software. So tasks were divided between QAs, Backend and frontend devs. Now a single dev can work on both sides and even test their code E2E without needing someone else. (T&C apply)* 
 The process changed from sprints to 2–3 days, from 2–3 people down to a single person. This shift doesn’t sit well with Agile and its core manifesto.
 The Shift in Overall Structure
 Devs have taken the faster feedback cycle in agile for granted as ‘get feedback faster, so we think less upfront’ which is a big no-no with AI.
 As I already mentioned, with AI you need to plan ahead, design first and then go for the agentic development. The shift where AI gives 5k lines of code at an instant & there’s little way to actually read through and review each line, planning and iterating over it before shoving it in Agent input box is mandatory now.
 Of course you can test later, but you know the difference of results between 2 lines prompt vs a well-defined context & design info from agents. All the guidelines, design decisions, naming conventions, module segregations, need to designed before. You cannot wait for next sprint and put the mess in “What went wrong” section in retrospective.
 AI asks you to be agentic with your thinking, and if you mess up, it’s on you. (that’s why I said it is like each dev have their own micro-waterfall phase going on with AI.) 
 Now, with AI, the middle management with the ceremonious roles of Product Owners, Scrum masters is not needed as a lean team of 5 with AI doesn’t need the fluff around them, which will actually slow them down with unnecessary meetings, discussions and review/planning.
 Big orgs are already shedding the middle managements as they realise the cost of shipping speed being slow is much more costly than anything else. (ahem ahem, Amazon) 
 And this change has came into my org as well, teams were made leaner, smaller cycles (we don’t follow agile fortunately) and reduced fluff all around and genuinely it feels better to work now coz I can work faster and report my results directly to a single person and not in a meeting with discussions to 10 unknown people which I see for 2 hours every month.
 That’s all I wanted to say, overall Agile was a manifesto of opinions, and now with AI, those opinions don’t matter anymore.
```

```text
Ain’t scared of losing my Tech job to AI
```

```text
State Machines in Computer Science
 State Machines In Computer Science 
 From ATMs to Servers, State machines are everywhere, so what’re they?
 A typical State Machine Diagram (by author) 
 A state machine is a model where computation is expressed as a set of discrete states and deterministic transitions between them.
 At any point in time, the system is in exactly one state. All behaviour is defined by how the system moves to the next state based on input and current state.
 This sounds theoretical until you realize that most systems you trust daily are silently doing exactly this.
 Examples include network protocol parsers (like the HTTP servers), database transaction managers, UI event loops, and operating systems. Everywhere at the core, there is somewhere a state machine running with going from state A to state B. 
 Why State Machines Appear in Real Systems
 State machines are not an academic preference. They appear because real systems must deal with partial progress, failures, retries, and concurrency with retries as well.
 Without an explicit notion of state, systems tend to devolve into:
 Boolean flag explosions
 Deeply nested conditionals
 Implicit deep coupling

 State machines force time and order to be explicit. It is a structured approach to solve the complex systems of high data processing, and handling different conditions under a set of deterministic rules and Input-Output.
 HTTP Servers as a Concrete Example (What I learnt)
 An HTTP server is not just a simple “handle request, return response”.
 At the protocol level (TCP here), it is a sequential machine processing a stream of bytes.
 A simplified lifecycle looks like:
 Idle ( waiting for connection… )
 Reading input ( request, headers, body, trailing headers )
 Parsing protocol data
 Validating correctness ( correct format, clear separation with \r\n, etc )
 Processing application logic
 Writing response
 Returning to idle

 Each step depends on the completion of the previous one. Skipping or merging states introduces ambiguity and bugs.
 Determinism Over Cleverness
 A useful property of state machines is determinism. (Unlike LLMs lol)
 Given the same state and the same input, the next state is predictable. We define the rules, the inputs for each state, the error state and the output for the next state.
 This matters because:
 Debugging becomes reproducible
 Logging gains meaning
 Failures can be classified by state
 Recovery strategies can be state-specific

 When engineers say a system is “ hard to reason about ”, it usually means state is implicit or scattered or quite complex to break down into predictable small steps.
 Error is Just Another State (Not something else)
 enum State {
 READING,
 PARSING,
 VALIDATING,
 PROCESSING,
 WRITING,
 ERROR, // is also a state
 COMPLETED
} When thinking in this mental model, every output/condition that a system can reach is another state, an error, exception, whatever, everything is a  STATE .
 In robust systems:
 Invalid input is a valid transition
 Timeout is a valid transition
 Cancellation is a valid transition

 Modelling errors as states ensures they are handled deliberately, not as surprises.
 We treat everything equally. 
 void transition(State expected, State next) {
 if (state != expected) {
 throw new IllegalStateException(
 "Invalid transition from " + state
 );
 }
 state = next; //move on to next state
} State Machines as a Design Tool
 class StateMachine {
private State state = State.IDLE;
 void handle(Input input) {
 switch (state) {
 case IDLE -> state = State.READING;
 case READING -> state = read(input)
 ? State.PARSING
 : State.ERROR;
 case PARSING -> state = parse(input)
 ? State.VALIDATING
 : State.ERROR;
 case VALIDATING -> state = validate(input)
 ? State.PROCESSING
 : State.ERROR;
 case PROCESSING -> state = State.WRITING;
 case WRITING -> state = State.COMPLETED;
 case ERROR -> recover();
 case COMPLETED -> state = State.IDLE;
 }
 }
 private boolean read(Input input) { return true; }
 private boolean parse(Input input) { return true; }
 private boolean validate(Input input) { return true; }
 private void recover() { state = State.WRITING; }
} State machines are not only an implementation detail. They are also a design tool. 
 When designing a component, ask:
 What states can this component be in?
 What inputs are valid in each state?
 Which transitions are illegal?
 What state represents completion?
 What state represents failure?

 If these answers are unclear, the design is not done yet. 
Go back to drawing board and rethink the approach or requirements.
 Code written after answering these questions is usually simpler, not more complex.
 Visual Models Beat Mental Models on scale
 Engineers often keep state machines in their head.
 This works until the system grows.
 Drawing the state machine makes illegal transitions visible. It exposes missing states. It reveals accidental cycles.
 This is why protocol specifications are full of state diagrams. They are executable documentation for humans.
 That’s why we have so many RFCs that define the states, inputs and outputs for many different technologies.
 We have one for HTTP/1.1, 2 and 3 and they are long, like LOOOOONG ! 
 RFCs as a design document ( https://www.ietf.org/process/rfcs/ ) 
 Good vs Bad Practices
 Common failure patterns  include:
 Encoding state in booleans instead of enums ( STARTED, PROCESSING, DONE )
 Mixing multiple state machines into one (Keep coupling loose)
 Allowing transitions from anywhere to anywhere (should follow a simple standard path)
 Handling errors outside the state model (they are the state!)
 Letting time dependent behaviour leak into logic (be strict on how to handle retries, exceptions, timeouts, etc)

 These problems compound as systems scale.
 State Machines Scale Better Than Abstractions
 As systems grow, abstractions multiply.
 State machines resist this tendency by anchoring behaviour in time and sequence.
 They do not prevent complexity, but they localise it.
 If something fails, you can easily check which state caused the issue and why, everything is partitioned into perfect smaller white boxes. 
 A system built on explicit state transitions is easier to extend than one built on implicit assumptions. 
 Final Thoughts
 If you have ever rewritten a system because it became unmaintainable, there is a good chance the real issue was unmanaged state built on vague requirements. 
 State machines are not something old and forgotten. They are the lowest stable abstraction for systems that must run reliably under imperfect conditions.
 Most good engineers eventually rediscover them, like I did while building my own small HTTP Server.
This is the basis of all compiler designs, programming language designs, etc.
```

```text
Using Blockchain in Digital Identity and Access Management Systems : A shift from Centralised to Self-Sovereign Identity
 Introduction 
 Identity management, often known as identity and access management, or IAM, is the act of controlling who can access certain types of information, technology, or services after completing a login process on a website or app. IAM is utilized in many different contexts, such as when a person accesses websites for personal use or when an employee uses technology at work.
 But as the world is advancing, the older, less-secure identity management systems have been prone to attacks like data breaches, large-scale hacks and information leaks, hence, there have been increasing regulations about how personal data is collected, stored, used and shared.
 Also, there’s no digital equivalent of a driver’s license, birth certificate or a social security number that can prove a person’s online presence and his/her identity. For every organisation, platform and application, the user needs to create a new identity, which in turn becomes problematic and cumbersome as we will see below.
 Fortunately, these issues may be successfully resolved by blockchain identity management technology, which improves security, efficiency, data correctness, and accessibility. Blockchain identification solutions are gaining traction because they provide a secure and affordable means of managing digital identities. Users save their credentials and ID information in a decentralized identity wallet application; the blockchain makes it possible to instantaneously verify this information without contacting the issuer. Users have more control over their personal data using ID wallets.
 Issues with conventional IAM systems 
 Lack of Centralised View 
 IAM systems are generally scattered across multiple applications and platforms, making it difficult to have a holistic insight to the bigger picture.
 Federated Systems offer easier options like Single Sign-On (SSO), but the risk is if the password is stolen, all the logged in accounts across platforms are at a risk of exposure.
 Even, if using Centralised Systems, they are also prone to data attacks as all the data is kept at a single place, which makes it easier for the attacker to gain access to the personal user data.
 Expensive KYC/AML Checks 
 Users, third parties, and verification organizations are all involved in Know Your Customer onboarding procedures. Financial institutions must adhere to anti-money laundering procedures as a basic necessity, and nearly all nations have stringent AML laws. In 2022, global spending on AML/KYC data and services reached around $1.6 billion.
 Conventional methods for confirming a user’s identity, documents, and background are frequently laborious, costly, and manual for all parties involved — especially the KYC company, which needs more resources to swiftly handle the demands of its clients in a variety of industries, such as banks, healthcare providers, and immigration authorities.
 Data Control and Ownership 
 Since users use and log in to multiple platforms and websites everyday, it is a tough task to know what data is being shared with them and what they are sharing with other partners without our consent. We hear details about millions of records of user data leaked in a data breach, these reports are a common occurrences in today’s world and this is the issue that the end-user does not have ownership and control over his/her Personal Identifiable Information(PII).
 User-Password Fatigue 
 An average person keeps around 100 login accounts in his/her lifetime. Handling and creating new accounts become fatiguing to remember and manage that often leads to phishing attacks.
 Lack of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) 
 Many IAM systems lack strong authentication methods like MFA, making it easier for unauthorized users to gain access to sensitive data.
 Introduction of Blockchain in the IAM landscape (Digital Identity Blockchain and Self-Sovereign Identity) 
 Digital Identity Blockchain is a revolutionary approach to managing and securing our digital identities. It leverages the power of blockchain technology to create a decentralized, secure, and transparent system for individuals to control their identities and share information selectively. Basically using blockchain is creating a system of self-sovereign IAM systems.
 Here’s a number of features over traditional IAM systems that are present in Digital Identity Blockchain:
 Identity Anchors: Known as “identity anchors,” blockchain serves as a safe ledger in which people may record their distinct online personas. Names, dates of birth, and other verifiable traits are among the relevant information contained in these anchors.
 Self-Sovereign Identity: People are in total control of the anchors that define who they are. They are in charge of deciding what data to include, who may access it, and why.
 Decentralized Verification: Blockchain technology allows for safe and transparent identity verification via a network of nodes, doing away with the need for centralized authority. This lowers the possibility of fraud and removes single points of failure.
 Enhanced Security: The authenticity and integrity of identification data are guaranteed by the cryptographic properties of blockchain. Since it is nearly hard to tamper with records, identity theft and data breaches are avoided.
 Interoperability: Standardized protocols make it possible for various blockchain systems to easily exchange identity information and communicate with one another. This encourages broader adoption and makes it easier to establish an international identity ecosystem.

 We can see that using blockchain in the IAM systems makes it more secure, scalable and also gives more control to the end-user in consenting to the type and amount of information he/she wants to share to other platforms.

 There are mostly two aspects of self-sovereign identity systems or basically Digital IAMs using Blockchain:
 Digital Identifiers(DIDs)
 Verifiable Credentials(VCs)

 Let’s have a look on both of them individually!
 Understanding Decentralised Identifiers (DIDs) 
 dock.io 
 Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs) are a new type of digital identifier that aims to give individuals and organizations more control over their online identities. Unlike traditional identifiers (email, passwords, usernames) , which are often managed by centralized authorities, DIDs are self-owned and controlled by the individual or organization itself.
 DIDs enable true self-sovereign identity — lifetime portable digital identity for any person, organization, or thing that can never be taken away. 
 In brief working of a DID 
 On the blockchain, a person’s or an organization’s DID serves as their unique character string. This document includes details about the DID owner, including public keys, related services, and additional pertinent information.
 DIDs are not managed or issued by a central authority, in contrast to traditional identifiers. DID owners are in total control of the information in their DID document, including what information to include and who can view it. DIDs use cryptography to make sure the data they store is authentic and accurate.
 Benefits of DIDs over traditional Identifiers 
 Enhanced privacy: DIDs provide users the ability to decide what data they choose to share with various apps and services.
 Enhanced security: DIDs based on blockchain technology are very impervious to hacking and data intrusions.
 Better user experience: DIDs make it possible for users to interact with different services and platforms in a pleasant and seamless way.
 Decreased dependence on third parties: People are no longer able to manage their identities through centralized authority.
 Increased interoperability: DIDs can function on many blockchain systems thanks to standardized protocols.

 Understanding Verifiable Credentials(VCs)
 Verifiable Credentials (VCs) represent a new category of digital credentials that utilize blockchain technology to provide a transparent, safe, and interoperable means of issuance and verification for individuals and organizations. Compared to conventional paper-based credentials, which are frequently vulnerable to fraud, loss, and inefficiency, they represent a major improvement.
 In brief working of a VC 
 Organizations, such as universities, governments, and businesses, can issue VCs to individuals. These VCs contain information about the individual and their qualifications, achievements, or other relevant data. VCs are stored on the blockchain, which ensures their tamper-proof and verifiable nature. Individuals can access their VCs through secure wallets.
 Individuals can present their VCs to service providers or other entities seeking verification. VCs can be easily verified using blockchain technology to confirm their authenticity and integrity.
 Relationship between DIDs and VCs 
 Each DID can have multiple Verifiable Credentials associated with them that are digitally (cryptographically) signed by their issuers like a government driver’s licensing department. DID owners store the credentials themselves on their phones and don’t have to rely on a single provider like Facebook or Google.

As we already know that DIDs are unique identifiers that are owned and controlled by the individual user and since they are not tied to any central authority that makes them more secure and private than traditional identifiers.
 Here are two examples of Verifiable Credentials (VCs) that can be linked to a DID: a university degree and a course certificate. VCs are digital credentials that can be issued and verified using blockchain technology. They provide a secure and tamper-proof way to share credentials with others.
 By storing DIDs and VCs on the blockchain, blockchain-based IAM systems can create a more secure, private, and efficient way for users to manage their identities and access privileges.
 Not everything should be mined: Why PII should never be stored on a blockchain?
 Since blockchain is immutable, once stored information can never be altered. Adding Personally Identifiable Information (PII) to a blockchain would lead to issues with data compliance and privacy laws like GDPR’s “right to be forgotten”.
 For example, if a hacker is able to crack the encryption in the next 10 years (highly unlikely though), he will be able to access all the data. Hence never store PII like driver’s license number, back account, social security number, etc.
 (Note: Many times PII is confused with Personal Information. Personal information or data can be anything related to an individual and may reveal its identity. However, on the other hand, personally identifiable information is any data related to an individual user to specifically identify a particular individual. All in all, not all personal information is PII, but all PII is personal information that can be used to identify a particular individual. )
 Some real-world applications of Blockchain in IAM systems.
 uPort: uPort is an open-source Ethereum based blockchain solution. An identity is created through an app on the user’s phone. This app holds all the data linked to the identity. It also holds the private keys, used to sign attestations and share them with others. Storing the data locally provides control over and access to the identity for the user and enforces them to provide consent before others can use the information.
 EverID: EverID is a user-centric, SSI and value transfer solution based on blockchain technology and the cryptographic underpinnings of that system. In contrast to uPort, EverID’s decentralized system is used to store and confirm user identity data, documentation and bio metrics. EverID facilitates verification of users by multiple third-parties and allows the secure transfer of value between members of the network. This means that claims made by users are provable. The decentralized architecture of the platform also provides personal data ownership which can only be accessed by the user. The individual’s data is recorded in a manner that allows the individual control how it is shared, with whom and for how long (persistence).
 Sovrin: Sovrin network is a public permissioned distributed ledger. This means that users can see the transactions but not necessarily initiate transactions. As a result, the need for proof-of-work is removed. This shows that they use a distributed consensus protocol which focuses more on security and scalability. This solution meets also the requirement of minimalization by enabling selective disclosure of claims using zero-knowledge proofs. Therefore no data will be shared without the user’s consent and this gives the identity owners control over their digital identity.

 Conclusion
 Digital Identities and traditional IAMs are one of the oldest and hardest problems on the internet. In Physical world, we can use our physical credentials to prove who we are, but it is not possible on the internet, for example, there’s no digital equivalent of a driver’s license, or a birth certificate to login to a website, or gain access to any platform, even in federated systems. We have to store and create hundreds of passwords and accounts which is prone to attacks.
 W3C standard for digital identities 
 The long going two problems of creating a standardized way of assigning digital identities and to verify the digital signatures of the credential owners is solved by blockchain as it provides a platform which no one owns, but everyone uses, hence it is safe, trustworthy and immutable.

 We can see its applications already in many places, and it will only grow from here onwards, not only making anyone’s online presence much more secure, but also seamless across organisations, platforms and applications.
 Blockchain to the rescue!
```
